# Lesson 06 — Mastering Chords, Rhythm and Strumming

**Course:** Absolutely Understand Guitar — Scotty West  
**Theme:** Playing a chord is the easy part. Switching in time is the skill. And strumming is not about stopping and starting your hand — it is about what you choose to hit.  
**Key promise:** The strumming and rhythm concepts in this lesson, combined with a timekeeper, will get you to a stage-ready level of rhythm in far less time than practicing without one.

## 1. Quick review — the chord practice formula

Before anything new, the formula from lesson 5 one more time:

```
Practice chords in pairs
Back and forth every four beats
Non-stop for five minutes at a time
WITH a timekeeper
As slow as you need
Think ahead — focus on the NEXT chord, not the current one
Listen — make sure it actually sounds clean
```

The key insight that everything else builds on:

> **Switching between chords with the beat is the hard part — not forming the chord itself.**

If you practice without a timekeeper, your rhythm will never develop into something you can actually use on stage or in a band. This is not optional advice.

---

## 2. Choosing a timekeeper

When you go looking for a timekeeper, the most important question is: **will it satisfy you?**

If you do not enjoy practicing with it, you will not use it. And if you do not use it, your rhythm will not develop.

### Two questions to ask about any timekeeper

**Question 1 — How does it sound?**

A cheap machine that sounds like a video game is not going to be fun to practice with. You want something that sounds reasonably close to a real human playing the drums. The closer to real it sounds, the more satisfying it will be to practice against.

**Question 2 — How many beat patterns does it have?**

Every song you will ever play has a slightly different drum pattern. Hold out for a machine with **at least 100 different patterns**. That way you will almost always be able to find something that matches the feel of whatever song you are working on.

### What to budget

In 1999 terms Scotty recommends the Yamaha PSR 79 in the $100 to $250 range. The specific model does not matter today — any modern digital keyboard with a built-in drum machine, or a free phone app, will do the job. The principle is the same:

- More fun than a metronome
- Can be plugged into an amplifier or stereo to match the volume of your guitar
- Shows you which beat of the pattern you are on, not just the tempo
- 100 or more beat patterns

> Check secondhand — a keyboard collecting dust in someone's house can be as good as a brand new one for this purpose.

## 3. How to count in four

About **80 percent of all popular music** is counted in repeating groups of four beats. You already have an intuitive feel for this from a lifetime of listening to music.

Counting out loud:

```
One   Two   Three   Four   One   Two   Three   Four...
```

Every time beat number one arrives, that is where you switch chords in the beginning stages of practice. You hold the chord for the whole four beats, strum it once as beat one hits, then use the remaining beats to form the next chord.

---

## 4. The progressive challenge system

This is the core practice method. Start at the slowest level and work your way up as each level becomes comfortable. Do not rush.

### Level 1 — Whole notes (one strum every four beats)

Strum the chord once on beat one. Hold it. Use beats two, three, and four to prepare your fingers for the next chord. Switch on the next beat one.

```
Beat:   1    2    3    4    1    2    3    4
Strum:  C              →   G
        strum  [form next chord here]  strum  [form next chord]
```

This gives you the maximum possible time — four full beats — to get your fingers in position.

### Level 2 — Half notes (one strum every two beats)

Strum on beat one AND on beat three. Now you only have two beats to form the next chord.

```
Beat:   1    2    3    4    1    2    3    4
Strum:  C         C         G         G
```

### Level 3 — Quarter notes (one strum every beat)

Strum on every single beat. Now you only have one beat to form the next chord.

```
Beat:   1    2    3    4    1    2    3    4
Strum:  C    C    C    C    G    G    G    G
```

### Level 4 — Eighth notes (half beat strumming)

Strum on every beat AND on every half beat between the beats (the "ands"). Now you have half a beat to make the switch.

```
Beat:   1  +  2  +  3  +  4  +  1  +  2  +  3  +  4  +
Strum:  C  C  C  C  C  C  C  C  G  G  G  G  G  G  G  G
```

This is the level **most songs are actually strummed at**. Getting here is the real target.

### Level 5 — Sixteenth notes (quarter beat strumming)

Count each beat as four subdivisions: **one E and A, two E and A, three E and A, four E and A**. Strum 16 times per measure. You have one quarter of a beat to make the switch.

```
Count:  1  e  +  a  2  e  +  a  3  e  +  a  4  e  +  a
```

This is the level of very fast strumming. Most songs do not require this but practicing here — even briefly — makes the half beat level feel easy.

### The two variables

You have two ways to make things harder:

1. **Move to the next strum subdivision** (whole → half → quarter → eighth → sixteenth)
2. **Speed up the timekeeper tempo**

Use them together. Once you can do level 4 at a slow tempo, speed up the tempo rather than jumping to level 5. When level 4 at a fast tempo feels solid, then try level 5.

### Summary table

| Level | Name | Strums per chord | Time to switch |
|-------|------|-----------------|----------------|
| 1 | Whole notes | 1 per 4 beats | 4 full beats |
| 2 | Half notes | 1 per 2 beats | 2 beats |
| 3 | Quarter notes | 1 per beat | 1 beat |
| 4 | Eighth notes | 2 per beat | Half a beat |
| 5 | Sixteenth notes | 4 per beat | Quarter beat |

## 5. Note values and how they relate to strumming speed

These are the standard note value symbols you will see in music notation and chord charts.

| Symbol name | Duration in a 4 beat bar | Strum rate |
|------------|--------------------------|------------|
| Whole note | Lasts all 4 beats | Once every 4 beats |
| Half note | Lasts 2 beats | Once every 2 beats |
| Quarter note | Lasts 1 beat | Once every beat |
| Eighth note | Lasts half a beat | Twice per beat |
| Sixteenth note | Lasts quarter of a beat | Four times per beat |

These will come up again when reading music and tablature is covered in lessons 9 and 10.

## 6. How to practice bar chords the same way

Bar chords are harder to form than open chords, but the practice method is **identical**. Use the same five-level system.

Example: practicing C major in the C form against G major in the E form (bar chord, 3rd fret).

Start at level 1 (whole notes) with the tempo set very slow. The bar chord will be difficult and ugly at first. That is expected. Keep going.

> "There was a time when I didn't think I would ever be able to do that. I can go to the quarter beat now."

The only difference with bar chords is that building the hand strength takes longer. It will realistically take **about a year** from when you start seriously practicing bar chords before they start sounding consistently clean. That is normal. Do not stop.

## 7. Your daily practice structure

When you sit down to play, split your time into two parts:

### Part 1 — Grunt work (first 20 to 30 minutes)

Do the technical work first while your focus is fresh. In order:

1. **Chromatic scales** (5 to 10 minutes) — use as a warmup, one finger per fret, up and down, with the timekeeper running. Play in time.
2. **Chord pairs** (5 to 10 minutes each pair) — pick two chords you are currently working on and run the five level system. Then pick another two pairs.

### Part 2 — Play (remaining time)

Work on actual songs you enjoy. This is where all the grunt work pays off. Do not skip the grunt work to get here faster — that just delays when the fun part becomes genuinely satisfying.

> "If you put the grunt work in at the beginning, the fun part will come that much sooner."

## 8. Left-handed players — a note

Many left-handed players make the decision to learn right-handed. Once you get used to it, most people report it stops feeling unnatural. The practical advantages are significant:

- Guitar shops stock very few left-handed instruments
- All chord charts, tab, and fingering diagrams are written for right-handed orientation
- Finding a teacher or other players to learn from is much easier

If you do choose to play left-handed, you will need to mentally mirror every fingering diagram you ever encounter, and finding instruments will always involve more effort. Both approaches are valid. Just make an informed choice early.

## 9. Right hand technique — alternate strumming

The right hand rule from the chromatic scale exercise in lesson 3 applies equally to strumming chords:

> **Always alternate your strumming direction: down, up, down, up, every single stroke.**

Most beginners default to all downstrokes. Some default to all upstrokes. Neither works at speed.

The reason is physical. Your brain controls your arm as a pendulum — it fires much more efficiently when the movement is regular and alternating than when it has to start and stop. Down up down up is a natural mechanical rhythm. Two down strokes in a row is a stumble.

**Rule:**
- **Every beat** = downstroke
- **Every half beat (the "and")** = upstroke

```
Count:     1    and    2    and    3    and    4    and
Pick:      ↓     ↑     ↓     ↑     ↓     ↑     ↓     ↑
```

Your hand moves in this pattern even when you are not hitting the strings on every stroke. The motion stays regular. The contact is selective. This is the key.

## 10. How strumming patterns actually work

This is one of the most important physical insights about strumming, and most people never figure it out on their own.

### The misconception

Most beginners think strumming patterns are created by moving the hand at different speeds — fast for short notes, slow for long notes, pausing between them.

### The reality

> **Your hand never changes speed. It moves back and forth absolutely regularly, like the pendulum of a clock. What changes is whether you make contact with the strings on any given stroke.**

Some strokes hit the strings. Some strokes whiff right past without contact. The pattern of hits and misses is what creates the strumming pattern.

```
Hand always moves:  ↓  ↑  ↓  ↑  ↓  ↑  ↓  ↑
Strings hit:        ↓     ↓  ↑     ↑  ↓
Strings missed:        ↑        ↓        ↑
```

This is why strumming with a pendulum hand and listening carefully is so important. Stopping and restarting your hand breaks the rhythm. Missing a string intentionally does not.

## 11. Reading a strumming pattern

Scotty's own notation system for strumming patterns:

| Symbol | Meaning |
|--------|---------|
| **D** | Downstroke that hits the strings |
| **U** | Upstroke that hits the strings |
| **●** | Missed stroke (hand moves but does not hit strings) |
| **● with arrow** | Missed upstroke specifically |

A four beat bar in this notation looks like this at the eighth note level:

```
Beat:    1   +   2   +   3   +   4   +
Hand:    ↓   ↑   ↓   ↑   ↓   ↑   ↓   ↑
Contact: D   ●   D   U   ●   U   D   U
```

Where D means the downstroke made contact, U means the upstroke made contact, and ● means the hand moved but did not touch the strings.

## 12. A worked example of a common strumming pattern

One of the most frequently heard patterns in popular music sounds like this when described by duration:

```
long  short  long  short  short  long  short  long  short  short
```

Or in Morse code style:   **—  ·  —  ·  ·  —  ·  —  ·  ·**

In Scotty's notation across one bar of four beats:

```
Beat:      1    +    2    +    3    +    4    +
Hand:      ↓    ↑    ↓    ↑    ↓    ↑    ↓    ↑
Contact:   D    ●    D    U    ●    U    D    U
```

Read it stroke by stroke:

- Beat 1 downstroke: **hit** (long)
- Beat 1 upstroke: **miss** (this creates the long feeling — the chord rings)
- Beat 2 downstroke: **hit** (short)
- Beat 2 upstroke: **hit** (short)
- Beat 3 downstroke: **miss**
- Beat 3 upstroke: **hit** (long — ringing again)
- Beat 4 downstroke: **hit** (short)
- Beat 4 upstroke: **hit** (short)

The key is that even on the missed strokes, your hand is still moving in its regular pendulum motion. The rhythm is maintained by the clock-like hand movement. The pattern is created by choosing which strokes make contact.

> A missed downstroke in the middle of a pattern is the part that trips most people up. The hand keeps going — it just does not hit the strings. Keep the pendulum swinging.

## 13. Key quotes

> "Music without rhythm is only fooling yourself that you are playing the song. If you cannot play it in time, you cannot play it. Period."

> "The only way to really develop a stage-usable professional relationship with rhythm is to practice with some kind of timekeeper machine."

> "Your hand is your clock. Think of it as the pendulum on a clock — just going very regularly back and forth with the beat."

> "You do not make contact with the strings on every single stroke. Sometimes your hand just whiffs right past the strings and you do not hit them."

> "Put the grunt work in at the beginning. The fun part will come that much sooner."

> "If you are not practicing with a timekeeper, you may not be completely wasting your time — but you are wasting half of it."
