## 1. The core philosophy

### Music is a language

The single most important idea in the entire course. Music functions in our lives exactly like a language — it communicates, it has structure, and learning it mirrors the process of learning any spoken language.

> **If music is a language → the guitar is a typewriter.**

The guitar is just the tool — the "dumb machine" — you type the music out on. The music itself comes from your mind. Most people approach it backwards: they go straight to the instrument before understanding the language, and that is exactly why they get stuck.

**The analogy in full:**

| English language | Music |
|---|---|
| Language (English) | Language (music) |
| Typewriter / keyboard | Guitar |
| Alphabet (26 letters) | Musical alphabet (12 notes) |
| Words | Chords, scales, intervals |
| Novelist / journalist | Musician / songwriter |

Once you understand how the English language works, figuring out a typewriter takes about an hour. Same deal with the guitar — lesson 3 covers the entire instrument in roughly one hour.

### Music is not a set of rules

It is a set of *observations*. Humanity has been experimenting with sound for tens of thousands of years. What works has been written down. You don't have to reinvent it — you just have to learn the order in which it was discovered.

> "You can go over in the corner and flop around and try to reinvent western music for yourself, but probably all you'll ever do is waste a lot of time."

### The two reasons people resist music theory

1. **It's usually taught as rules** — this course teaches it as observations you're being let in on.
2. **Fear of complexity** — music theory sounds intimidating, but it's a pathetically simple subject once explained in the right order.

## 2. Why most people quit

### The statistic

For every 75 people who start an instrument at some point in their lives, **only 1** continues playing it for the rest of their life.

The reason is not:
- Lack of talent
- Not practising enough

The reason is: **lack of satisfaction.**

People fail to get the payback they deserve for the work they put in. We don't continue doing things that bring us no benefit.

### The key to lifelong satisfaction: learning songs by ear

Every person Scotty knows who still plays music for life can do one thing: **learn songs just by listening to them.**

Why this matters:
- Sheet music for obscure artists is often unavailable
- ~70% of guitar tabs on the internet are inaccurate (sometimes completely wrong)
- Without being able to play the music you love, you can't satisfy yourself

To play by ear with *any* song (not just easy ones), **you have to know what you're doing.** That is what this course teaches.

## 3. Two things a real player must do

### Thing 1 — Get to the end of the song

Playing riffs and bits is not playing. A musician is only as good as their song list.

> "Real artists produce real works of art. You don't walk into an art gallery and see paintings on the wall that are only half finished."

**Pitfall to avoid:** The Stairway to Heaven intro problem — thousands of people "know" the first 20 seconds of a song and nothing else. That's not playing the song.

**Choose doable material.** Don't attempt songs that are too far over your head:
- Stairway to Heaven is about a 4/10 in difficulty — but it requires ~1–2 years before you can truly play it
- Metallica, Slayer, thrash metal — not realistic without significant work first
- Start with simple 2–3 chord songs and build up

**Musical difficulty scale (rough guide):**

<img src="https://github.com/prabhavpokharel/understand-guitar/blob/main/img/lesson-01/guitar-difficulty-scale.png?raw=true" width="500">

### Thing 2 — Check yourself out

After finishing a song, stop and ask: **"How did I do?"**

The best practice habit: **record yourself every time you play.**
- Doesn't need to be high quality — a basic voice recorder works
- Listen back like it's someone else playing
- You often can't hear your actual sound while you're focused on your fingers
- This is how you identify what needs fixing

> "You need to get outside of your own ego and be able to listen to your playing."

## 4. The six main areas of music

All music — every style, every era — involves only six things. Master the understanding of all six and you can evaluate any performance, including your own.

> Another way to think of it: music is a language and it has **six parts of speech.**

| # | Area | Official term | One-line description |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Pitch** | Pitch | What notes you play and what patterns you arrange them in — the largest area |
| 2 | **Rhythm** | Rhythm | The control of time — beat, tempo, pulse |
| 3 | **Sound quality** | Timbre (TAM-ber) | The quality of sound produced — different from pitch |
| 4 | **Loudness** | Dynamics | How loud or soft you play at any given moment |
| 5 | **Physical technique** | Technique | How you physically interface with the instrument |
| 6 | **Reading music** | Notation | Reading and writing sheet music |

### Area 1 — Pitch (the biggest area)

Pitch = high and low, but high and low *what*? **Notes.**

Everything you came here to learn lives under pitch:

<img src="https://github.com/prabhavpokharel/understand-guitar/blob/main/img/lesson-01/pitch-subheadings.png?raw=true" width="500">

These are all **patterns of notes** — like words spelled from the musical alphabet.

### Area 2 — Rhythm

The control of time. All music has a pulse. You must keep with the beat or nothing else matters.

- Covered in depth in Lesson 6

### Area 3 — Timbre

Same pitch, different sound quality. A piano and a human voice can sing the exact same note and sound completely different. That difference is timbre.

Affects: choice of instrument, electric vs acoustic guitar, amplifier, effects (distortion, chorus), EQ settings, string freshness.

> **Change your strings every 1–2 months.** Old strings lose tone so gradually you don't notice — until you put new ones on.

### Area 4 — Dynamics

Loudness. The official musical term you'll see in guitar magazines.

You must:
- Balance yourself with the other musicians
- Balance yourself to the size of the room
- Play loud in the loud parts, soft in the soft parts — within the same song

### Area 5 — Technique

The physical side: how you hold the instrument, how you use your fingers, how you interface with the machine. Not the starting point — the result of first understanding the language.

> Out of every 200 people walking around claiming to be guitar players, only **1** actually understands what they're doing.

- Covered fully in Lesson 3

### Area 6 — Notation

Reading and writing sheet music. Learning curve is comparable to learning to read a language:
- Starts with simple exercises (Mary Had a Little Lamb = "See the cat run" in English)
- Takes years of dedicated work to read fluently
- Required to reach level 10 (jazz/classical)
- Optional for rock and pop — tablature may be sufficient
