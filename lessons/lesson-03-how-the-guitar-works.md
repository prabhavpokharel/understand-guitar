## 1. Quick review of lessons 1 and 2

Before diving in, make sure you have these down solid:

- The musical alphabet has **12 notes**, not 7
- **B → C** and **E → F** are the two half step pairs with no sharp or flat between them
- A sharp (♯) means higher in pitch. A flat (♭) means lower in pitch
- A♯ and B♭ are two different names for the exact same note
- Memory trick for the half step pairs: **B C E F**

> Every single song in every single style of music you have ever heard is just a reshuffling of those 12 notes.

---

## 2. The six open strings

Your guitar has six strings. Going from the **thickest and lowest** in pitch up to the **thinnest and highest**, their names are:

```
String 6  →  E  (lowest pitch, thickest string)
String 5  →  A
String 4  →  D
String 3  →  G
String 2  →  B
String 1  →  E  (highest pitch, thinnest string)
```

**Important:** Guitars are numbered with string 1 at the top (high E) going down to string 6 (low E). This is backwards from how you should think about the instrument. Always think **low to high** — thick to thin — otherwise you will never understand how it works.

---

## 3. Why the strings are in alphabetical order

E A D G B E looks random. It is not.

Those are not random letters. They are **selected notes from the 12 note musical alphabet**, pulled out of an alphabetically ordered sequence with other notes sitting in between them that you are not seeing.

The full sequence running through the open strings looks like this:

```
E  F  F#  G  G#  A  A#  B  C  C#  D  D#  E  F  F#  G  G#  A  A#  B  C  C#  D  D#  E
↑                 ↑              ↑              ↑           ↑                          ↑
E                 A              D              G           B                          E
(string 6)     (string 5)    (string 4)    (string 3)  (string 2)               (string 1)
```

The open strings ARE in alphabetical order. You just cannot see it because the notes in between are invisible unless you are fretting them.

## 4. The core principle — four fingers four frets

> **This is the single most important fact about understanding the guitar as a physical object.**

<img src="https://github.com/prabhavpokharel/understand-guitar/blob/main/img/lesson-03/four-fingers-four-frets.png?raw=true" width="500">

The guitar was designed by and for human beings who have **four fingers** on their fretting hand. So whoever originally designed the standard tuning made sure that each pair of adjacent strings is exactly **four notes apart**.

Why four notes? Because that is exactly how many notes you can play with your four fingers before running out. When you reach your pinky on one string, the very next note higher up is sitting right on the open next string. You just switch strings and keep going.

It works exactly like a piano keyboard. On a piano you can see the notes spread out in a straight line from low to high. On the guitar the same sequence of notes is spread across six strings in a logical grid. The principle is identical.

```
Piano:   | C | C# | D | D# | E | F | F# | G | G# | A | A# | B | C |
Guitar:  Open → Fret 1 → Fret 2 → Fret 3 → Fret 4 → (switch string) → Open → ...
```

<img src="https://github.com/prabhavpokharel/understand-guitar/blob/main/img/lesson-03/fretboard-first-position.png?raw=true" width="500">

## 5. Walking through the fretboard string by string

Here is exactly what you are playing when you walk up the neck from the lowest note to the highest, string by string.

### String 6 — Low E

| Position | Note |
|----------|------|
| Open | E |
| Fret 1 | F |
| Fret 2 | F# / G♭ |
| Fret 3 | G |
| Fret 4 | G# / A♭ |
| → next open string | A |

You ran out of fingers on G#. The very next note is A. That is why string 5 is tuned to open A.

### String 5 — A

| Position | Note |
|----------|------|
| Open | A |
| Fret 1 | A# / B♭ |
| Fret 2 | B |
| Fret 3 | C |
| Fret 4 | C# / D♭ |
| → next open string | D |

Notice fret 3 is C, not B#. This is one of the half step pairs — B goes straight to C with nothing in between.

### String 4 — D

| Position | Note |
|----------|------|
| Open | D |
| Fret 1 | D# / E♭ |
| Fret 2 | E |
| Fret 3 | F |
| Fret 4 | F# / G♭ |
| → next open string | G |

You just hit another E on fret 2 of the D string. Count how many notes you have played from your open low E string to here: 13 notes. You are now **one octave above** where you started, and you are roughly halfway across the neck.

### String 3 — G

| Position | Note |
|----------|------|
| Open | G |
| Fret 1 | G# / A♭ |
| Fret 2 | A |
| Fret 3 | A# / B♭ |
| → next open string | B ← only 3 notes here, not 4 |

**This is where the exception happens.** See section 6.

### String 2 — B

| Position | Note |
|----------|------|
| Open | B |
| Fret 1 | C |
| Fret 2 | C# / D♭ |
| Fret 3 | D |
| Fret 4 | D# / E♭ |
| → next open string | E |

Fret 1 on B string is C, not B# — again the B to C half step pair.

### String 1 — High E

| Position | Note |
|----------|------|
| Open | E |
| Fret 1 | F |
| Fret 2 | F# / G♭ |
| Fret 3 | G |
| Fret 4 | G# / A♭ |

You have now gone from your open low E to your pinky note on the high E string. That is **29 notes** and approximately **two and one third octaves** without moving your hand from the first four frets.

## 6. The G and B string exception

All other pairs of adjacent strings are tuned **four notes apart**. The G string and B string are only **three notes apart**. This is intentional and permanent. It will affect your fingering patterns for the rest of your guitar playing life.

### Why was this done?

The guitar is a **polyphonic instrument** — meaning it can play multiple notes simultaneously (chords). About two hundred years ago, instrument builders discovered that tuning G and B just three notes apart instead of four suddenly made a huge number of chord fingerings physically possible that had previously been impossible to reach.

The trade off was a slight inconsistency in the tuning logic. It was worth it.

> **Polyphonic** means many notes at once. Guitar, piano, harp — polyphonic. Trumpet, saxophone, flute — monophonic (one note at a time only).

### What this means practically

Whenever you are working with a pattern that crosses from the G string to the B string, something shifts by one fret to account for the missing note. This shows up in chord shapes, scales, and especially in the chromatic scale exercise below.

## 7. Key numbers to know about the guitar

| Fact | Number |
|------|--------|
| Notes in an octave | 12 |
| Notes from open string to 12th fret | 13 (one full octave) |
| Notes accessible in first position (open strings + first 4 frets) | 29 |
| Range covered in first position | ~2⅓ octaves |
| Total range of the guitar | ~4 octaves |
| Total range of a piano | ~7 octaves |
| Low E string to high E string | exactly 2 octaves |

### The 12th fret

The 12th fret is marked with **double dots** on most guitars. It is the only fret with two dots. It marks exactly one octave above your open string. Whatever note your open string is, the note at the 12th fret is the same letter name, one octave higher.

### Low E and high E are not the same note

A very common misconception. Your low E string and high E string are both named E but they are **two full octaves apart**, not one. If you only knew one octave separated them, now you know the real answer.

## 8. How to hold the guitar

### The test

Before you play a single note, there is one physical test that tells you whether you are holding the guitar correctly:

> **Can you touch all four frets at once comfortably?**

If you cannot, your position is wrong.

### What most people do wrong

Most people rest the guitar on their **right leg** with the neck angled downward. When you try to reach all four frets from that position your shoulder hunches, your elbow tucks into your body, and your wrist bends at an extreme angle. It feels wrong because it is wrong.

### The correct way to sit

Place the guitar on your **left leg** with your knees slightly apart. The body of the guitar sits centered in front of you and the neck angles slightly upward. Your fretting arm hangs naturally, your elbow has room to move, and reaching all four frets feels easy.

This is how classical guitarists have always sat. They get more out of the instrument than anyone else precisely because they figured this out.

<img src="https://github.com/prabhavpokharel/understand-guitar/blob/main/img/lesson-03/guitar-holding-wrong-vs-right.png?raw=true" width="500">

Think of it this way. When you stand up and put a guitar strap on, the strap naturally pulls the guitar to the center of your body with the neck angled up. Sitting on your left leg replicates that same position without a strap.

### Thumb position

Your thumb should rest **behind the neck**, roughly opposite your middle finger. Not wrapped over the top of the neck the way most beginners instinctively do it.

## 9. The chromatic scale exercise

> **This is the single most important exercise anyone will ever give you on the guitar.**

<img src="https://github.com/prabhavpokharel/understand-guitar/blob/main/img/lesson-03/index-pinky-shift.png?raw=true" width="500">

A chromatic scale means playing all 12 notes in sequence, one at a time, moving up or down by half steps. On the guitar in first position it means walking up the fretboard string by string.

### Why this exercise matters

Playing a chromatic scale is literally typing your way through the musical alphabet on the typewriter. If you can do it cleanly in both directions, you have control of the instrument. Everything else is built on top of this.

### How to play it going up

Before you start, touch all four frets at once to confirm your hand position. Then:

```
String 6 (low E):  Open → Fret 1 → Fret 2 → Fret 3 → Fret 4
String 5 (A):      Open → Fret 1 → Fret 2 → Fret 3 → Fret 4
String 4 (D):      Open → Fret 1 → Fret 2 → Fret 3 → Fret 4
String 3 (G):      Open → Fret 1 → Fret 2 → Fret 3  ← stop here, NOT fret 4
String 2 (B):      Open → Fret 1 → Fret 2 → Fret 3 → Fret 4
String 1 (high E): Open → Fret 1 → Fret 2 → Fret 3 → Fret 4
```

One finger per fret: index finger on fret 1, middle on fret 2, ring on fret 3, pinky on fret 4.

On the G string you only go to fret 3 (not fret 4) because of the G to B exception. If you played fret 4 on the G string you would be playing the same note as the open B string — a repeated note. Skip it.

### How to play it going down

Start on your pinky note on the high E string and reverse the process. On each string, the open string is the **last note you play** before switching down to the next lower string.

```
String 1 (high E): Fret 4 → Fret 3 → Fret 2 → Fret 1 → Open
String 2 (B):      Fret 4 → Fret 3 → Fret 2 → Fret 1 → Open
String 3 (G):      Fret 3 → Fret 2 → Fret 1 → Open    ← only 3 frets here
String 4 (D):      Fret 4 → Fret 3 → Fret 2 → Fret 1 → Open
String 5 (A):      Fret 4 → Fret 3 → Fret 2 → Fret 1 → Open
String 6 (low E):  Fret 4 → Fret 3 → Fret 2 → Fret 1 → Open
```

## 10. Alternate picking

From the very first day, train your picking hand to use **alternate picking**: down stroke, up stroke, down stroke, up stroke, every single note.

<img src="https://github.com/prabhavpokharel/understand-guitar/blob/main/img/lesson-03/alternate-picking.png?raw=true" width="500">

Most beginners default to all down strokes. Some default to all up strokes. Both are dead ends. Speed on the guitar is physically impossible without alternate picking because you are wasting half of your available pick motion.

The whole skill of playing the guitar comes down to coordinating your picking hand (down up down up) with your fretting hand (four fingers across four frets). That is essentially it.

## 11. Moving up the neck — finger shifts

In first position you get to use the open strings as your fifth note on each string. Further up the neck the open strings are irrelevant and you cannot rely on them anymore. Now your four fingers have to somehow cover five notes.

The solution: **one finger plays two notes**. This is called a finger shift.

### The box

At any position on the neck, your four fingers occupy a group of four frets. This is called **the box**. In reality the box you need to think about is six frets wide, because your index finger will sometimes reach one fret below the box and your pinky will sometimes reach one fret above it.

### Index shifts (going up)

When ascending the scale and you reach your pinky note on a string, instead of stretching your pinky out one more fret (awkward), you:

1. Reach your **index finger** one extra fret onto the next string
2. Play that note
3. Scoot your index finger back into its normal position on that string
4. Your index finger has now played two notes

```
Ascending:  ... → pinky note → index reaches out one fret on next string → index shifts back → continue up
```

This keeps your hand position stable and avoids forcing your pinky into an awkward stretch away from your hand.

### Pinky shifts (going down)

When descending and you reach your index finger note on a string, instead of awkwardly reaching your index finger out below the box, you:

1. Reach your **pinky** one extra fret onto the previous string
2. Play that note
3. Scoot your pinky back into its normal position on that string
4. Your pinky has now played two notes

```
Descending: ... → index note → pinky reaches out one fret on previous string → pinky shifts back → continue down
```

Your pinky is your weakest finger and will resist this at first. Practice it anyway. This is one of the fundamental coordination skills of guitar playing.

### The G to B exception applies here too

When going up the scale across the G to B string transition, **no index shift is needed** because the strings are already only three notes apart. If you tried to do a shift there you would repeat a note.

When going down the scale across the B to G string transition, **no pinky shift is needed** for the same reason.

```
Going up:    index shifts on every string transition EXCEPT G → B
Going down:  pinky shifts on every string transition EXCEPT B → G
```

### Summary table

| Direction | Shift type | Exception |
|-----------|------------|-----------|
| Ascending | Index shift | Skip on G → B |
| Descending | Pinky shift | Skip on B → G |
