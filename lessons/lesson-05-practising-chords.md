## 1. Quick review of lesson 4

Before moving on, these things from lesson 4 should be solid:

- A chord is several different notes played simultaneously
- There are about 45 chord types, 12 of each type, but only 5 finger forms
- The five forms are E form, A form, D form, C form, and G form
- Bar chords let one finger shape play all 12 keys by sliding up the neck
- Every chord has a root note — the lowest note that shares the chord's name
- Do not play notes lower than the root

The six chord types being studied right now:

| Type | Sound |
|------|-------|
| Major | Happy |
| Minor | Sad |
| Dominant 7th | Bluesy |
| Minor 7th | Sad and bluesy |
| Major 7th | Peaceful and mellow |
| Power chord (5th) | Aggressive, raw |

## 2. Chord names have three parts

Every chord has a name. That name always has three components, though the third one is often left out. Learning to read a chord name is a skill you need to develop alongside your finger technique.

### The three parts

| Part | What it tells you | Example |
|------|-------------------|---------|
| **1. Root note** | Which of the 12 notes is the anchor | B flat |
| **2. Chord type** | What kind of chord it is | major seven |
| **3. Form** | Which of the five finger forms to use | in the A form |

Put together: **B flat major seven in the A form**

The third part (the form) is often not written in song books and chord charts. When it is missing, you need to look at the chord picture and figure it out yourself. That is a skill you have to develop.

### Why chord names can get long

A chord type can take several words to describe. For example: minor seven sharp five flat nine. Add a sharp or flat root note and a form label and you can end up with a twelve word chord name like **B flat minor seven sharp five flat nine in the A form**. That is still just one chord.

You will not be dealing with anything that complex right now, but knowing the structure helps you read any chord name you encounter.

## 3. Working backwards through a chord name

The most reliable method for figuring out how to play any chord from its name is to **work backwards** through the three parts.

### Step 1 — Start with the form

The form tells you which **horizontal row** on your chord chart to look at. Knowing the form immediately eliminates four fifths of the chart.

### Step 2 — Then find the type

The chord type tells you which **vertical column** on your chart to look at. Now you can triangulate — form gives you the row, type gives you the column, and where those two intersect is your basic finger shape.

### Step 3 — Finally the root note

The root note tells you how far up the neck to move the bar chord. Start from the open position of that form (where the root is an open string) and count frets up the neck until you land on the note you need.

```
Chord name →  Work backwards  →  Form (row) + Type (column) = basic shape
                               →  Root note = how far up the neck to move it
```

## 4. Worked examples

### Example 1 — A flat minor seven in the E form

Work backwards:

1. **Form:** E form. Look at the E form row on your chart.
2. **Type:** Minor seven. Look at the minor seven column. Where row and column intersect is your basic shape — E minor seven open position.
3. **Root note:** A flat (G#). Starting from open E minor seven, count up frets. E is open, F is fret 1, F# is fret 2, G is fret 3, G# or A flat is fret 4.

**Result:** Bar on the 4th fret, E minor seven shape above it.

On a chord chart: write the number 4 next to the chord box to indicate the fret position.

### Example 2 — E seven in the D form

Work backwards:

1. **Form:** D form. Look at the D form row.
2. **Type:** Dominant seventh. Triangulate to D seven open position — that is your basic shape.
3. **Root note:** E. In D form the open root is D. Count up: D is open, D# is fret 1, E is fret 2.

**Result:** Bar on the 2nd fret, D seven shape above it. Do not play the two lowest strings — they are below the root.

Note: In D form the bar does not need to press all six strings hard. The three upper strings are covered by your other fingers and the two lower strings are muted. The bar really only needs to play the root note on the D string. Think of it less as a full bar and more as just fretting that one root note.

### Example 3 — E flat major seven in the C form

Work backwards:

1. **Form:** C form. Look at the C form row.
2. **Type:** Major seven. Triangulate to C major seven open position.
3. **Root note:** E flat (D#). In C form the open root is C (on the A string, 3rd fret). Count up from C: C# is one fret up, D is two up, D# or E flat is three up. So the root moves to the 6th fret on the A string.

**Result:** Bar on the 3rd fret (three frets below the root note), C major seven shape with the root at the 6th fret. Low E string muted.

## 5. The reverse skill — naming a chord from a picture

The other skill you need is the exact opposite: looking at a chord diagram and figuring out what it is called.

Same process, same three parts, same backwards logic.

1. Find the root note position in the diagram
2. Identify which form it is from the root note location and finger shape pattern
3. Match the finger pattern to a chord type on your chart
4. Count the frets to identify the root note name

---

## 6. How to figure out which form a chord is in

The root note location is your fastest shortcut.

| Root note is on | Possible forms |
|-----------------|---------------|
| Low E string (same fret as bar) | E form |
| Low E string (different fret from bar) | G form |
| A string (same fret as bar) | A form |
| A string (different fret from bar) | C form |
| D string | D form |

### A form vs C form (both have root on A string)

In **A form**: the root note started as an open string, so when you bar it, the root sits on the **same fret** as the bar.

In **C form**: the root note started as a fretted note (not an open string), so when you bar it, the root sits on a **different fret** than the bar — usually several frets above it.

### E form vs G form (both have root on low E string)

In **E form**: same principle as A form. Root sits on the same fret as the bar.

In **G form**: root sits on a different fret from the bar.

## 7. The E form major seven — a special case

The E form major seven chord has an unusual bar shape that is worth understanding before you encounter it.

### Why you cannot play it in open E position

The major seven shape requires an open high E string as part of its voicing. If you tried to move the root note down to the open low E string, the note on the high E string would need to be *below* the open string — which is physically impossible. You cannot play a note lower than the open string on the same string.

For this reason, the E form major seven cannot actually be played with E as the root. The **lowest key it works in is F**. The root begins at the first fret on the low E string.

### The angled bar

Most bar chords use your index finger lying flat across all strings on a single fret. The E form major seven is different. Your index finger needs to:

- Press the low E string at one fret with the tip of the finger
- Press the high E string at the fret **below** using the fleshy part further back on the finger

This means your index finger is on a slight angle rather than lying straight across. It is one of a small number of chords where this is required.

## 8. Chord variations

Some chord shapes have alternative fingerings that are equally correct. You will see these in your chord chart booklet.

| Chord | Variation |
|-------|-----------|
| E7 | Can be played with two notes or with all four notes — both are valid |
| A7 | Same — two note version or full four note version |
| E minor seven | One finger version (easiest chord on the page), or add two more notes, or add one more on top — three different valid voicings |
| A minor seven | Two finger version or extended four finger version |
| G major | The note on the B string is optional — some versions include it, some do not |

These variations all produce the same chord type and spelling. The differences are in voicing — which combination of the available notes you choose to include. Once you understand chord structure more deeply later in the course, the reason why multiple fingerings are valid will become obvious.

## 9. The sad truth about chords — and the practice method that fixes it

### The sad truth

> **Playing** a chord is the easy part. **Switching** between chords is the hard part.

Most people spend all their practice time learning to form individual chord shapes. But in any real song, you are constantly switching from one chord to the next in rhythm. If you cannot get your fingers from chord A to chord B in about half a second while keeping time, it does not matter how perfectly you can hold any individual shape.

The goal is not to know how to play a chord. The goal is to **get there instantly**.

### The practice formula

```
Practice chords in pairs
Back and forth every four beats
Non-stop for five minutes at a time
WITH a drum machine or timekeeper
As slow as you need to be
Think ahead to the next chord
Listen to yourself as you play
```

### Why pairs

Practicing five chords at once divides your attention too thin. Pick two chords and go back and forth between them until the transition is automatic. Then pick two more. One pair at a time.

### Why four beats

About 80 percent of all popular music is counted in repeating groups of four. You already have an intuitive feel for this. Use it. Count one two three four one two three four — switch chord on beat one of each cycle.

### The five minute rule

Who cares if you can play a chord five times in a row? Songs last three to five minutes. You need to be able to sustain the chord switching for that long without your hand giving out.

Some of this is pure **physical conditioning**. Bar chords in particular require hand strength that most beginners simply do not have yet. Treat it like going to the gym. Push until you feel the fatigue, then stop before you hurt yourself.

> Guitar players are vulnerable to tendonitis and carpal tunnel syndrome — the same injuries that affect tennis players and typists. Do not push through sharp pain. Discomfort is normal. Pain is a signal to stop.

### Think ahead

This is crucial. Do not think about the chord you are currently playing. By the time you are playing it, it is too late to adjust it. Push your mind forward and be thinking about the **next** chord while your fingers are still holding the current one.

### Listen to yourself

Make sure the chord is actually ringing clearly. If strings are buzzing or muting out, you need to know about it. Listening critically to your own playing while you practice is not optional — it is the whole point.

## 10. Get a timekeeper — this is not optional

> "It is pretty much pointless to practice without a timekeeper."

This is one of the strongest statements in the whole course and it is completely serious.

Rhythm is one of the six main areas of music. Without a timekeeper you have no external reference to measure your sense of time against. Your internal sense of rhythm will drift, speed up, slow down, and never develop into something stage-usable.

### Options

| Timekeeper | Notes |
|------------|-------|
| **Drum machine** | Best option — fun to practice with, gives you a full beat pattern not just a click, can be plugged into an amp or stereo |
| **Keyboard with built-in drum patterns** | Most cheap portable keyboards have a drum machine built in — perfectly usable |
| **Metronome** | Works, but just a click — less engaging, hard to hear at volume |
| **Smartphone app** | Any drum machine or metronome app works fine |

### How to use it

1. Set the tempo slow enough that you can make the chord switch cleanly
2. Practice at that tempo for five minutes
3. Speed it up slightly
4. Repeat

Your fingers will get faster over time. The machine gives you something concrete to chase.
