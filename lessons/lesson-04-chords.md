## 1. What a chord is

A chord is when you select **several different notes** from the musical alphabet and play them all **simultaneously**.

That is it. Nothing more complicated than that at its core. When you strum a guitar and hear multiple strings ring out together, you are playing a chord.

---

## 2. The numbers — 45 types, 12 of each, 5 finger forms

Before anything else, get these three numbers into your head. Everything in this lesson flows from them.

| The number | What it means |
|------------|---------------|
| **45** | The total number of chord types that exist (major, minor, 7th, diminished, augmented, suspended, and so on) |
| **12** | How many versions exist of each type — one rooted on each of the 12 notes in the musical alphabet |
| **5** | The number of finger shapes physically possible on a guitar |

### Why 12 of everything

Because there are 12 notes, any pattern in music can be rooted on any one of those 12 notes. So there are 12 major chords, 12 minor chords, 12 seventh chords, and so on for every chord type. This is also why there are exactly **12 keys** you can play in.

### What is a key

A key just means one note is acting as the anchor or nucleus for everything happening in a song. Once you figure out which of the 12 notes is that nucleus note, you know what key the song is in. That is all you need to know about keys for now.

---

## 3. Why there are only five finger forms

If there are 12 different major chords, why do most guitarists only know five of them? And why is it always the same five?

The answer is not that those five are the most common or the easiest. The answer is the same one from lesson 3:

> **The guitar has profound physical limitations as to what combinations of notes your fingers can actually reach.**

On a polyphonic instrument, not every combination of notes you might want to play is physically possible. You cannot make your fingers do 12 different and completely unique shapes to play 12 different major chords. When you try all the possible ways to finger a major chord on a guitar, you end up with **exactly five shapes**. That is the ceiling. There are no more.

But here is the key insight: **this does not mean you can only play five chords.** It means that with five shapes and the concept of the bar chord, you can play all 12 versions of any chord type, in five different ways each.

## 4. Chords are words with spellings

This is the most important idea in this entire lesson.

> A chord is a word in the language of music. It has a spelling. When you put your fingers on the neck to play a chord, you are getting the typewriter to produce the word you are trying to spell.

Every chord has a specific set of notes that must be present for it to qualify as that chord. Those notes are its spelling. The notes:

- Do not have to be in any particular order
- Do not have to be in any particular octave
- Can be repeated as many times as the instrument allows

### Example: E major chord

The spelling of an E major chord is **E, G#, B**.

When you play the open E major chord shape on a guitar, here is what is actually sounding across all six strings:

| String | What is playing | Note |
|--------|-----------------|------|
| 6 (low E) | Open string | E |
| 5 (A) | 2nd fret | B |
| 4 (D) | 2nd fret | E |
| 3 (G) | 1st fret | G# |
| 2 (B) | Open string | B |
| 1 (high E) | Open string | E |

You are playing three E notes (all one octave apart from each other), two B notes, and one G#. Every single note is either an E, a G#, or a B. The spelling checks out perfectly. That is why it works.

### Example: A major chord

The spelling of an A major chord is **A, C#, E**.

Every note you play when you strum an open A chord is either an A, a C#, or an E. Check it on your fretboard chart. There is no mystery.

> You could almost figure out where to put your fingers yourself if you know the spelling and you have a fretboard chart in front of you. Did anyone teach you specifically how to spell the word "cat" on a typewriter? No. You knew the language, you knew c-a-t was the spelling, and you figured out the rest. Chords work exactly the same way.

## 5. The five forms

The five finger shapes are named after the open chord they most obviously resemble. You will see these names everywhere in guitar education. Memorise them.

| Form | Open chord it resembles | Root note location |
|------|------------------------|-------------------|
| **E form** | Open E major | Low E string (string 6) |
| **A form** | Open A major | A string (string 5) |
| **D form** | Open D major | D string (string 4) |
| **C form** | Open C major | A string (string 5) |
| **G form** | Open G major | Low E string (string 6) |

These five forms are not just for major chords. Every chord type (major, minor, 7th, minor 7th, major 7th, power chord) has a version in each of the five forms.

## 6. Bar chords — how to reach all 12 keys

### The problem with just moving your fingers up

Take an open E major chord. The notes in that chord are E, G#, and B. Now suppose you want to play F major, which is the very next chord higher (E and F have no sharp between them — remember B C E F).

To play F major you need to raise all those notes up by one. The obvious thought is to just move your three fingers up one fret. But when you do that and strum all six strings, the chord sounds wrong and eerie instead of happy and normal.

Why? Because you did not raise **all** the notes. You only raised the three that your fingers were on. The open strings — your low E, high B, and high E — are still sitting there playing the notes they were tuned to. Half the chord went up and half stayed behind. You are now playing two different chords at the same time.

> The open strings are not a non-event. They are part of the chord just as much as the notes your fingers are fretting. You have to raise them too.

### What a bar chord does

A bar chord uses your **index finger** laid flat across all six strings to act as a new nut. It raises the pitch of every string including the ones you are not fretting with your other fingers. Now when you move everything up one fret, all six notes go up together, and the chord sounds correct.

### The process for making any bar chord

1. Take the open chord shape you want to move
2. Rearrange your fingers so that your **index finger is free** (use middle, ring, and pinky for the chord shape instead)
3. Move everything up the neck by however many frets you need
4. Lay your index finger flat across all strings at the fret below your other fingers
5. Now all strings are raised — open strings included

### One shape, all 12 chords

Once you understand bar chords, a single form can reach every chord in the musical alphabet just by sliding up the neck.

Using the E form as an example:

```
Open position → E major
1st fret       → F major
2nd fret       → F# major
3rd fret       → G major
4th fret       → G# / Ab major
5th fret       → A major
6th fret       → A# / Bb major
7th fret       → B major
8th fret       → C major
9th fret       → C# / Db major
10th fret      → D major
11th fret      → D# / Eb major
12th fret      → E major (one octave higher)
```

The same logic applies to every other form and every other chord type. Any form can play all 12 versions of any chord type.

So the full picture is:

> **5 forms × 12 keys = you can play every chord, in every key, five different ways.**

### Bar chords take time

Be realistic about this. Bar chords are physically demanding. It will likely take **one to two years** of regular practice before you can play them cleanly and switch between them quickly. That is normal. Do not get discouraged.

## 7. Why two versions of the same chord sound different

Play E major in the open E form. Then play E major as a bar chord in the A form at the seventh fret. They are both E major chords — same spelling, E G# B — but they sound noticeably different.

**Why they sound similar:** Same spelling. Both chords contain only E's, G#'s, and B's. Same word.

**Why they sound different:** Even though the spelling is identical, the arrangement is not. When you move up the neck:

- Some notes are now in a higher octave than they were before
- The order the notes appear across the strings has changed
- Which notes get repeated and which do not has changed

Same word, different voicing. Think of it like the same sentence read aloud by two different people in two different rooms. The meaning is the same but the sound is different.

## 8. The six chord types covered in this lesson

| Type | Sound and mood | Notes |
|------|---------------|-------|
| **Major** | Happy, bright, open | The foundation of most popular music |
| **Minor** | Sad, dark, heavy | Same five forms as major |
| **Dominant 7th** | Bluesy, tense, unresolved | Very common in blues and classic rock |
| **Minor 7th** | Sad and bluesy at the same time | E minor 7 is the easiest chord on the whole page — one finger |
| **Major 7th** | Peaceful, mellow, pastoral | Softer pop and jazz flavoured sounds |
| **5th chord / Power chord** | Driving, aggressive, raw | Heavy metal, hard rock, alternative — upper strings muted |

All 45 chord types can be played in all five forms across all 12 keys. Today you are learning six of those 45 types. That is enough to start playing songs.

## 9. Power chords and the B string exception

Power chords (also called 5 chords) have the same finger shape whether you play them in the E form or the A form. You just shift the whole shape over one string and it works the same way. This makes sense because the strings are all tuned four notes apart.

However, when that same shape crosses onto the **B string**, the G to B exception kicks in again. Because G and B are only three notes apart instead of four, the note that lands on the B string has to be raised by **one fret** to maintain the correct relationship between the notes.

This is the same principle that caused the finger shift rule in the chromatic scale. Any time a note moves onto the B string, it shifts up one fret to compensate for that missing note in the tuning.

This also explains why the open A form major chord has a slightly different shape from the open E form major chord. The same relationship is at work. A note ended up on the B string and had to move up one fret, which changed the visual shape of the chord even though the spelling stayed the same.

## 10. Root notes

Every chord has a **root note**. The root is the note the chord is named after. It is the anchor, the foundation, the lowest note in the chord that shares the chord's name.

> "Every house wants to be built on a good foundation. So does every chord."

### Root note locations by form

| Form | Root note is on |
|------|----------------|
| E form | Low E string (string 6) |
| A form | A string (string 5) |
| D form | D string (string 4) |
| C form | A string (string 5) |
| G form | Low E string (string 6) |

### Why some strings are muted (marked with an X)

As a general rule, **do not play notes lower than the root note**. Notes below the root muddy the foundation of the chord and make it sound undefined.

This is why:

- A form chords have an X on the low E string (playing it would be below the root)
- D form chords have X's on both the low E and A strings (both are below the root)
- C form chords have an X on the low E string (the root is on the A string)
- G form chords use all six strings (the root is on the low E string, nothing is below it)

When you see an X on a chord diagram, it is not arbitrary. It is there because that string would put a note below the root and undermine the foundation of the chord.
