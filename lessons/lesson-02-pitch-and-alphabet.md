## 2. The science of pitch — frequency

### What is pitch?

Pitch = high and low. But high and low *what*? **Notes.** And notes are all about **frequency**.

### Sound waves

Everything making noise is vibrating and sending waves through the air. Frequency is the number of wave peaks that pass a fixed point (like your ear) per second. Measured in **Hertz (Hz)** — cycles per second.

<img src="https://github.com/prabhavpokharel/understand-guitar/blob/main/img/lesson-02/sound-waves-low-high.png?raw=true" width="500">

| Wave type | Frequency | Pitch |
|---|---|---|
| Peaks far apart | Low (few per second) | Low pitch |
| Peaks close together | High (many per second) | High pitch |

### The guitar as a sound wave generator

A musical instrument is nothing more than a **controlled sound wave generator**. The guitar generates sound waves at very precise, controllable frequencies — that is literally all it does.

A guitar string vibrating back and forth puts the wave peaks where they are. The speed of that vibration determines the frequency, which determines the pitch.

> **Speed of string vibration → frequency → pitch**

## 3. Three factors that control pitch on the guitar

| Factor | How it works | Where you see it |
|---|---|---|
| **1. Mass (thickness)** | Thicker strings vibrate slower → lower pitch | Low strings are thicker than high strings |
| **2. Tension** | Tighter strings vibrate faster → higher pitch | Tuning machines (tuning pegs) |
| **3. Length** | Shorter string vibrates faster → higher pitch | **Fretting** — the most important factor in playing |

### Factor 3 — length — is the big one

When you press a string against a fret, you shorten the vibrating length of the string. Shorter = faster vibration = higher pitch. Moving your hand up the neck makes the string shorter and shorter, which is why the pitch gets continuously higher.

```
Open string:       |————————————————————| full length = lowest pitch
1st fret:          |——————————————————|   shorter = slightly higher
5th fret:          |——————————————|       shorter still = noticeably higher
12th fret:         |——————————|           half length = exactly one octave higher
```

> This is what fretting *is* — you're controlling the vibrating length of the string to change pitch.

### Tuning tip

> **Always use an electronic chromatic tuner.** Tuning by ear is too difficult for beginners and wastes time. Get an auto-chromatic electronic tuner and use it every time you pick up the guitar.

## 4. The musical alphabet — 12 notes, not 7

### The three most common wrong answers

| Wrong answer | Why people say it | Why it's wrong |
|---|---|---|
| **8** | Do Re Mi Fa Sol La Ti Do from school | "Do" is repeated — only 7 unique notes, not 8 |
| **7** | A B C D E F G | That's only the white keys on a piano |
| **Sharps and flats are different** | The way music is usually taught | Completely wrong — black keys are equal to white keys |

### The correct answer: 12

Seven white keys + five black keys = **12 notes**. That's it. That's all of western music.

> The piano keyboard's black and white colour coding is purely a **visual convenience** — so your eye can quickly navigate to a note. It has nothing to do with musical importance. Black keys are used just as often as white keys.

### The 12 notes in order

```
A  →  A#/Bb  →  B  →  C  →  C#/Db  →  D  →  D#/Eb  →  E  →  F  →  F#/Gb  →  G  →  G#/Ab  →  A (octave)
```
<img src="https://github.com/prabhavpokharel/understand-guitar/blob/main/img/lesson-02/piano-keyboard-12-notes.png?raw=true" width="500">

All 12 notes are a **half step** apart from each other as you move through them in sequence — with two exceptions (see section 5).

### What is an octave?

An octave is the distance from one note up to the next note with the **same letter name**. For example, from one A up to the next A higher — that spans 12 half steps, and the 13th note is where it starts over.

> That's why Scotty drew 13 boxes on the chart — 12 notes + the octave repetition at the top.

Notes repeat in octaves infinitely in both directions. The guitar itself covers about **four octaves** of range.

## 5. Sharps, flats, and the half-step pairs

### What sharp and flat mean

They are just **directions** — nothing more. Like port and starboard on a boat.

| Symbol | Name | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| **♯** | Sharp | Higher in pitch (next note up) |
| **♭** | Flat | Lower in pitch (next note down) |

A♯ and B♭ are **two names for the exact same note**. Whether you call it one or the other depends on context (covered in a later lesson — for now, it doesn't matter).

### The two half-step pairs — memorise these

Most adjacent letter-named notes have a sharp/flat note between them (a whole step apart). But in **two places** there is no black key — the notes are only a half step apart with no note in between:

```
B → C     (no sharp/flat between them)
E → F     (no sharp/flat between them)
```

**Memory trick: B C E F — repeat it until it's automatic.**

```
B C E F   B C E F   B C E F
```

Every other pair of letter-named notes (A→B, C→D, D→E, F→G, G→A) is a **whole step** apart, with a sharp/flat note in between.

### Half step vs whole step

| Interval | Definition | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Half step** | The smallest interval — moving to the very next note (one fret) | B to C, E to F, or any note to its sharp/flat |
| **Whole step** | Two half steps — skipping one note (two frets) | A to B, C to D, F to G |

## 6. The 50 patterns — your musical vocabulary

All of music is built from **about 50 core patterns** selected and arranged from the 12-note alphabet. These are the "words" of the musical language.

### How the 50 break down

| Category | Number to learn | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **Intervals** | 13 | The most important — everything else is made of intervals |
| **Scales** | 14 | Sequences of notes in a pattern |
| **Chords** | ~20 | Notes played simultaneously |
| **Total** | ~50 | For popular music |

> To play jazz or classical, you'd need roughly **150 patterns** (triple the pop music vocabulary). This course focuses on the 50 you need for popular music.

### The analogy

| English | Music |
|---|---|
| 26-letter alphabet | 12-note musical alphabet |
| Selecting letters and arranging them | Selecting notes and arranging them |
| Words | Chords, scales, intervals |
| Nouns, verbs, adjectives... | Major chords, minor chords, scales... |
| ~171,000 words in the dictionary | ~50 patterns for pop music |

## 7. The four ways to know every pattern

This is the single most important framework in the course. Every pitch pattern — every chord, scale, and interval — must be learned **four different ways**. This is exactly how you already know every word in English.

> Most guitarists only know their patterns one way (way 4 — finger patterns). That is a dead end.

<img src="https://github.com/prabhavpokharel/understand-guitar/blob/main/img/lesson-02/four-ways-diagram.png?raw=true" width="500">

### Why way 3 (hearing) is the most important

Music is an *audio* art form. The most important thing about it is how it sounds. If you can't recognise a pattern by ear, you can never truly understand it — no matter how well you know the theory or the finger shapes.

> Way 3 is the whole world of **ear training** — the subject of the next section.

### Why way 4 alone is a dead end

Most self-taught guitarists only know their patterns as finger shapes. That's like learning to type without knowing how to spell. The typewriter doesn't write the book — the language in your mind does. Finger patterns are just the typing.

## 8. Ear training — the two most important words in music

### What ear training is

Working with a coach who teaches you **clues for recognising what each pattern sounds like**. You learn to identify chords, scales, and intervals by ear — the way you identify words in speech.

The proof that you know a pattern: **you can sing it back.**

> Ear training is the **second required course at every music school** — right after music theory. Most self-taught players have never even heard of it.

### Smart vs not-smart playing by ear

| Not-smart ear playing | Smart ear playing (trained ears) |
|---|---|
| Trial and error on the instrument | Recognise patterns the moment you hear them |
| Hours or days to figure out one song | Figure out a song while listening to it |
| Fails on 10 songs for every 1 success | Works on any song you want to learn |
| Can't touch complex music (Steely Dan, Dave Matthews, Phish) | No song is off limits |
| The guy playing you that Pearl Jam song didn't tell you how long it took | Scotty can figure out a Pearl Jam song in 3–5 minutes |

### What trained ears unlock

1. **Listen-learn** — figure out any song just by listening to it a few times
2. **Composing** — you can't really write music without trained ears
3. **Improvising** — this is almost entirely where improvisation comes from (see section 9)

### Your first ear training clue: major vs minor

| Chord type | Sound | Emotional feel |
|---|---|---|
| **Major chord** | Bright, open | Happy |
| **Minor chord** | Dark, closed | Sad |

Happy music uses more major chords. Sad/dark music uses more minor chords. This is one of 20 chord-recognition distinctions to develop. Other chords sound like fear, surprise, tension, or ecstasy — each has its own emotional fingerprint.

### The singing requirement

A good ear training coach makes you sing patterns back. Singing is the only proof that you actually know what something sounds like. If you can sing a pattern, you know it. If you can't, you don't — regardless of how well you know it on paper.

> Start simple: your coach plays a note, you sing it back. Then intervals. Then chords. Then scales. Then progressions. That is the full arc of ear training.

## 9. Improvisation — where it really comes from

Most people think improvisation comes from knowing more and more finger patterns. **It doesn't.**

Improvisation comes from **trained ears**.

### The proof

Scotty's demonstration:
1. He plays an improvised solo over a backing track → sounds impressive
2. He then sings the exact same solo — *before* playing it on the guitar
3. The solo came from his mind first. The guitar just translates it.

> **"You will never play a better guitar solo than you can sing."**

The process:
```
Trained ears → hear the melody in your mind → fingers find the notes on the guitar
```

Not:
```
Learn more finger patterns → hope something sounds good
```

### What this means for practice

Learning to sing intervals, chords, and scales is not just an ear training exercise — it is directly building your improvisation ability. Every hour of ear training is an hour of becoming a better improviser.
